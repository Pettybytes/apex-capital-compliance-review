# Pushback: Rethinking Automated Review

### Where's the AI component?
The brief implies a desire to use AI or LLMs to make compliance decisions. However, checking trades against a Limit of Authority matrix is a deterministic rules problem. In financial institutions that handle trades, relying on generative AI for regulatory decision making is extremely dangerous. The SEC and FINRA have been explicit that existing legal obligations apply fully to AI-enabled conduct. Regulatory bodies explicitly warn against the risks of hallucinations where a model generates inaccurate information presented as fact. If a system incorrectly clears a restricted trade, a firm cannot point to an AI hallucination as a defense for a supervisory failure. Using an LLM to make routing or clearing decisions introduces unacceptable hallucination risk and destroys audit ambiguity. At best, an LLM could be integrated to summarize complex trade logs or extract context for human reviewers, but it must never be used to make the final regulatory judgment.

### Why "Automated clearing, never the judgment?"
The architecture deliberately restricts auto-approval (L1) exclusively to unambiguous trades: Notional under $500k with absolutely zero mandatory flags. Some business stakeholders may request maximizing automation by auto-clearing specific low-risk flag combinations if the LOA loosely allows it. We must push back on this practice. Auto-approval requires ironclad proof in the audit log. If a regulator asks why a specific trade bypassed human review, the answer must be instantly reproducible. We enforce a strict zero-flags auto-clear policy because the risk of auto-approving these combinations invites deep audit investigations and major compliance breakdowns. We automate the clearing, never the judgment.

### Where do we go from here?
The brief asks us to automate the review process, but the current LOA matrix only lets us automate the triage. In the PoC, we automatically cleared 35% of the trades. The other 65% are simply sorted into a queue a human still has to work. This sorting is valuable and ensures no risky trades slip through, but it does not reduce the actual review burden. To genuinely shrink the human workload, we need a system that learns from past decisions. The most expensive analyst time is spent on L2, L3, and L4 escalations. If we track these manual decisions over a few weeks, we will find specific patterns that reviewers approve every single time. We can then use that data to propose new rules to Apex Capital and safely expand that 35% boundary.
